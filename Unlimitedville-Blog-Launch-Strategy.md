## Part 3: Blog Voice and Tone Guidelines

### Brand Voice for Unlimitedville Blog Content

**Voice (consistent across all content):**
Knowledgeable but not condescending. Unlimitedville is the neighbor who happens to know a lot about internet technology and explains it in plain English over the fence. The blog should feel like it was written by someone who has personally dealt with terrible rural internet and figured out the solution -- because that is the company's origin story.

**Core voice attributes:**

- **Plain-spoken.** No jargon unless it is defined immediately. "Latency" becomes "latency (the delay between clicking and something happening)." Technical accuracy matters, but accessibility matters more.

- **Empathetic.** The reader is frustrated. Their internet is slow, expensive, or both. Acknowledge that before jumping into solutions. Never talk down to someone for not knowing what 5G is or how a hotspot works.

- **Practical.** Every article should leave the reader with something actionable, even if they never become a customer. This builds trust and earns backlinks, both of which drive SEO.

- **Confident but not salesy.** UV's service sells itself on facts (unlimited data, no contract, 21-day trial). The blog should present those facts naturally within helpful content, not push them. The reader should feel like they discovered UV as a solution, not that they were marketed to.

### Tone Adjustments by Content Type

| Content Type | Tone Shift |
|---|---|
| Comparison articles (vs Starlink, etc.) | Balanced and fair. Acknowledge competitor strengths. Win on facts, not spin. |
| How-to / educational guides | Patient and thorough. Assume the reader is starting from zero. |
| Problem-focused articles (slow internet, etc.) | Empathetic first, solution-oriented second. Lead with "we get it." |
| Listicles / roundups | Efficient and scannable. Less personality, more utility. |

### Writing Style Rules

```
DO:
- Use short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max)
- Use subheadings every 200-300 words for scannability
- Include a table or comparison chart in every article
- Use real numbers (speeds, prices, data caps) whenever possible
- Write in second person ("you") to speak directly to the reader
- Front-load the answer -- give the takeaway in the first 100 words
- Include the primary keyword in the first paragraph naturally
- Use alt text on every image with relevant keywords

DO NOT:
- Use "we" or "our" outside of clearly marked UV mentions
- Write salesy CTAs in the middle of educational content
- Use filler phrases ("in today's digital world," "it goes without saying")
- Keyword stuff -- if a sentence sounds unnatural, rewrite it
- Make claims without backing them up (speed claims need source or context)
- Use exclamation points in body copy
- Bash competitors by name -- compare on facts only
```

### How to Subtly Point to Unlimitedville

The blog's primary job is to rank and capture organic traffic. Its secondary job is to introduce UV as a solution. Here is how to do both without undermining credibility:

**1. The "Natural Mention" pattern:**
When discussing cellular/hotspot internet as a category, mention Unlimitedville as one example: "Providers like Unlimitedville offer unlimited cellular data plans starting at $69/month with no contracts." This reads as informational, not promotional.

**2. The "What to Look For" pattern:**
List criteria for choosing an internet provider (no data caps, no contracts, money-back guarantee, US-based support). These criteria happen to describe UV perfectly. The reader connects the dots themselves.

**3. The "Bottom CTA" pattern:**
Every article ends with a brief, low-pressure call to action: "If you're in an area with cellular coverage, Unlimitedville offers a 21-day risk-free trial so you can test it before you commit." This is factual, helpful, and non-pushy.

**4. The "Author Bio" pattern:**
Each post includes a brief author/brand note: "This article is published by Unlimitedville, a wireless home internet provider serving rural America since [year]. We test every claim we publish." This builds authority without being promotional in the content itself.

### On-Page SEO Checklist for Every Article

```
- Title tag: Primary keyword + year + compelling modifier (under 60 chars)
- Meta description: Summarize the value prop in 150-160 chars with a micro-CTA
- URL slug: Short, keyword-rich (e.g., /blog/best-rural-internet-options-2026)
- H1: Matches or closely mirrors the title tag
- H2s: Use secondary keywords as subheadings
- First 100 words: Include primary keyword naturally
- Internal links: Link to at least 2 other UV pages (other blog posts + relevant service page)
- External links: Link to 1-2 authoritative sources (FCC data, speed test tools) for credibility
- Images: At least 2 per article with keyword-rich alt text
- Schema markup: Article schema on every post (headline, author, datePublished, publisher)
- Word count: Minimum 1,500 words for any article targeting competitive keywords
```

### Ongoing Content Cadence (Post-Launch)

After the initial 4 articles, publish 2 new articles per month targeting the remaining keyword clusters from the initial 10. Prioritize:

## Part 5: Competitive Positioning Summary

### Who Owns the SERP Right Now

The top organic results for rural internet queries in 2026 are dominated by review aggregators (Allconnect, Reviews.org, CNET, CableTV.com, HighSpeedOptions) and direct competitors (Nomad Internet, UbiFi, Starlink). Nomad Internet has the most active blog among direct competitors with dozens of articles targeting rural internet keywords.

### Unlimitedville's Content Gap

UV currently has zero blog content. The site has strong landing pages (rural internet, gaming, residential, plans) but no informational content competing for top-of-funnel or comparison queries. This means UV is invisible to anyone who is not already searching for the brand by name.

### The Opportunity

By publishing 4 high-quality, SEO-optimized articles at launch and maintaining a 2x/month cadence, UV can:

- Capture organic traffic from non-branded searches (people who have never heard of UV)
- Compete directly with Nomad Internet and UbiFi for informational queries
- Build topical authority that strengthens rankings across the entire domain
- Create a content moat that compounds over time as articles age and earn backlinks

The blog is not a nice-to-have. For a company with no retail presence and no brand awareness outside of existing customers, organic search is the highest-leverage growth channel available.
